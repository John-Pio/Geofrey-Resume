What I Learned Doing SEO on My Own Portfolio Site

I did a full SEO pass on this GitHub Pages portfolio. Here's exactly what was missing, why it mattered, and how I fixed it — written for designers and developers who want to make their own portfolio more crawlable without drowning in jargon.

Why a Portfolio Needs SEO at All

The common assumption is that a portfolio site doesn't need SEO because people find it through your social links or a direct referral. That's partially true — but it misses the point of what SEO actually signals. A well-structured portfolio tells Google (and any recruiter who pastes your URL into a sharing debugger) that you care about the details. For someone applying to an SEO content creator role, having a broken Open Graph setup is a contradiction.

This wasn't a vanity project. It was a proof of concept: can I set up the technical SEO foundations on a static GitHub Pages site correctly, and can I produce content that demonstrates I understand why each piece matters?

What Was Already There

Before the SEO pass, this site already had the basics: descriptive <title> tags on every page, <meta name="description"> on all pages, Open Graph tags (og:title, og:description, og:image), canonical links, and JSON-LD structured data covering Person, WebSite, ProfilePage, Article, and more. The profile image path was also fixed early — a broken image in structured data is worse than no image.

That gave a reasonable foundation. What it didn't have was everything else.

Fix 1: robots.txt

What was missing and why it matters

Without a robots.txt, crawlers have no guidance. Some bots may deprioritize sites that lack it. The fix was two lines: allow all crawlers, point to the sitemap. GitHub Pages serves it from the repo root automatically.

Fix 2: sitemap.xml

What was missing and why it matters

Without a sitemap, Google has to discover pages by following links. That works eventually, but it's slower and less reliable for small sites with few inbound links. The sitemap lists every URL with a priority and change frequency — including the project.html?id= query-string pages, which Google can crawl but benefits from being told about explicitly.

Fix 3: Custom 404 Page

What was missing and why it matters

GitHub Pages serves a generic error page on broken URLs. A branded 404 keeps users from bouncing immediately by giving them navigation options. It also signals that the site is maintained — a small but real quality signal.

Fix 4: meta author and Twitter Card Tags

What was missing and why it matters

Open Graph was already in place, but Twitter/X uses its own card system. Without twitter:card, twitter:title, and twitter:image, X falls back to OG tags — which mostly works, but leaves appearance inconsistent. The meta name="author" tag is a minor but complete-checklist item.

Fix 5: Internal Linking

The site's pages were mostly connected only through the nav. Internal links pass authority between pages and help crawlers understand which pages are most important. Three concrete additions: the home page now links to the process guide, the process guide footer links to both the work page and the about page, and the about page work highlights link to individual project pages.

None of these feel forced because they're genuinely useful for a real reader. The process guide footer saying "See the finished ad creatives" is a natural next step.

Fix 6: BreadcrumbList Schema on project.html

What was missing and why it matters

Project pages are dynamic — they load content via JavaScript based on a query string parameter (?id=project-one). Breadcrumb schema was added dynamically in the same script block that already updates the page title. Google can show these breadcrumbs in search results, making the result look more structured and the URL path clearer.

Fix 7: Content (Phase 3)

Technical SEO is table stakes. The real differentiator is content that earns indexing because it answers questions people actually search for. This article is part of that — along with the AI tools workflow breakdown and a practical ad creative checklist. Each article follows proper heading hierarchy, includes Article schema markup, has an internal link back to the work page, and targets keywords the process-guide.html already has some authority for.

The most important thing I learned: the technical fixes take an afternoon. The content takes longer, but it's what actually gets indexed and shared. If you're a designer trying to demonstrate SEO knowledge, writing clearly about what you did and why is more useful than a perfect Lighthouse score.

Final Thoughts

Every fix in this article was reversible, additive, and verifiable — no dark patterns, no keyword stuffing, no tricks. Just completing what was missing and documenting it clearly. That's what SEO content creation actually is: systematic, transparent, and useful.

The next step is submitting the sitemap to Google Search Console and requesting manual indexing for each new page. After that, it's a waiting game — and a content game.
