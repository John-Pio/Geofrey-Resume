Static Ad Creative Checklist: 12 Things to Review Before You Export

Catch problems before they become revision rounds. This checklist covers every layer of a static ad — from hierarchy and copy to compliance and file naming — in the order you should actually check them.

This checklist grew out of the 10-step static ad creative workflow documented elsewhere in this portfolio. It's designed for the moment between "compositing is done" and "I'm about to export." Run through it top to bottom. The items near the end are the ones most people skip, and they're the ones that create problems after delivery.

The 12-Point Pre-Export Checklist

01
Canvas dimensions match the platform spec

Meta feed static: 1080×1350px (4:5) or 1080×1080px (1:1). Stories: 1080×1920px. Google Display: varies. Export the right size, not whatever you happened to build the canvas at.

02
Text is readable at thumbnail size

Zoom out to 25% and read the headline. If you can't read it easily, the headline is too small or too low-contrast. This is the most common oversight that only shows up at feed size.

03
Safe zones are respected

Keep key content (headline, product, CTA) away from the outer 5–8% of the frame. Platforms crop or overlay UI elements near the edges on mobile. What looks fine in Photoshop may be partially hidden in-feed.

04
Visual hierarchy is clear in three seconds

A viewer's eye should travel: hook → product/visual → CTA. If the CTA is the most visually prominent element, the order is wrong. If the product is buried, the creative is broken.

05
Copy matches the approved brief

Check every word in the ad against the approved brief or client sign-off. Specifically: headline, subhead, CTA label, any supporting claims or offers. One wrong word can mean a revision round or a compliance issue.

06
No unsupported claims or misleading language

"Guaranteed results," "Number 1 in the world," "Cures X" — all of these can get an ad rejected or an account flagged. If the claim requires a source, either add the source or rewrite the claim. When in doubt, flag it with the account manager before export.

07
Logo is visible and clearspace rules are followed

Check the brand guidelines: minimum logo size, clearspace around the mark, and approved placements. A logo that's too small or overlapping another element is a brand violation, not just a design note.

08
Colors match the brand palette

Use the exact hex values from the brand guide, not eyedropper samples from a reference image. Color can drift through screenshots, compressed references, and monitor differences. Check against the spec.

09
Product cutout edges are clean

Zoom to 100% and check the edges of any cut-out product image. Fringe, halos, or rough masking edges read as amateur work at feed size. This is especially critical for products on white or light backgrounds.

10
All fonts are embedded or outlined

If the PSD uses system fonts or fonts that aren't embedded, anyone else who opens the file will see substitutions. Outline or rasterize text layers before sending the master file. Export as PNG/JPG for delivery, not PSD.

11
File is exported at the correct format and compression

Meta: PNG for graphics-heavy, JPG for photography. File size under 30MB (usually well under). Google: check the specific Display spec — some placements have strict file size caps. Export settings matter; don't just hit "Save for Web" with defaults.

12
File is named correctly and folders are organized

Use the naming convention: [Client]_[Campaign]_[AdName]_[Hook]_[Variable]_[Variant#].png. A clearly named file in a clearly structured folder is what separates a professional delivery from a confusing ZIP of random files. Don't underestimate how much this matters to the person receiving the work.

Print this list or keep it open in a second window during every final review pass. The items you're most likely to skip are 05 (copy check against the brief) and 12 (file naming). Both of those will create friction later if you miss them.
